# Budget Guide to Backpacking China

**Source:** Nick Wharton (goatsontheroad.com), long-running couple travel blog, backpacked China for 1 month and lived there for 13 months. Published March 2013, updated June 2023. Budget-focused, first-hand experience.

## Quick Facts

- **Daily budget:** $75/day for two people (comfortable level with private hostel room, good food, entrance fees, beer)
- **Best time to visit:** Spring (March-May) or autumn (September-early November)
- **Visa:** Tourist L-Visa, 30-day single-entry; apply in advance (no visa on arrival). Canadians ~$100, Americans ~$140
- **Pro tip:** Land in Hong Kong (no visa needed) and apply for the Chinese visa there

## Budget Breakdown

### Accommodation: $10-$30/night
- Hostels are excellent — clean, comfortable, great English-speaking staff, regular social events (dumpling-making nights, BBQs)
- Ask hostel staff to write directions in Mandarin for showing taxi/bus drivers
- **Top pick:** Yangshuo Culture House — owner Wei provides 3 home-cooked meals/day plus accommodation for ~$15/day/person

### Food: $0.50-$5/meal
- Street food (dumplings, steamed buns, cold/hot noodles, soup): pennies
- Restaurant meals: under $3 for amazing dishes
- Best enjoyed in large groups — order many dishes and share around the table
- "Chinese food is some of the best in the world and it's way better in China!"

### Entrance Fees: Average $15/person
- Great Wall of China: 45 Yuan (~$7.50)
- Forbidden City: 80 Yuan (~$13.50)
- Terracotta Warriors: 150 Yuan (~$25)
- **Student card gets 50% off all entrance fees**
- Great Wall "tour" from Beijing hostels: 280 Yuan (~$48) including pickup, drop-off, and lunch; self-guided, not tacky

### Alcohol: $0.30 big beer, $3 cocktails
- Tsingtao is the most popular but other brands are cheaper
- A case of 12 big bottles (650 mL): under $5
- Big beer at a restaurant: almost always under $1
- Baijiu (白酒): "toxic petroleum flavoured liquor" but the standard way to bond with Chinese men over dinner. "Gan bei!" (干杯 = cheers)

### Tipping
- **Do not tip.** There is absolutely no tipping culture in China. Taxi drivers, waiters — no one expects or understands tips. Just take your change.

## Must-See Cities (South to North)

### Hong Kong (香港)
Expensive but buzzing city with great restaurants, bars, vibrant nightlife. Don't miss ancient architecture, old towns, and outlying islands. Note: rooms tend to be small due to space constraints.

### Yangshuo (阳朔)
Rice paddies, farmland, rivers divided by massive limestone karsts. Rent a bicycle to explore the countryside. One of the most beautiful places in China.

### Guilin & Dragon's Backbone Rice Terraces (龙胜梯田)
Tour to Dragon's Backbone from Guilin: 130 Yuan/person for transport (arrange through hostel). Panoramic shimmering rice paddies etched into mountainsides over 500 years ago.

### Tunxi (屯溪区)
Old town in Anhui Province. Old architecture, narrow alleys, friendly people, great hostels. Rarely visited by tourists. Central district of Huangshan City — good side trip from Shanghai.

### Chengdu (成都)
Sprawling metropolis of ~15 million. Nice gardens and a restored old town. Main draw: Giant Panda Breeding Research Base with large enclosures. Note the authors' caveat: "be aware of the mistreatment of animals at some of their 'attractions'. Always do your research."

### Shanghai (上海)
Infamous skyline, old architecture, funky walking street. "Probably the coolest big city in China." Can be polluted. Excellent restaurants. Recommended hostel: Mingtown Etour International Hostel.

### Xi'an & Terracotta Warriors (西安)
Nice city with an interesting Muslim district worth visiting on its own. Terracotta army: over 8,000 soldiers, 130 chariots with 520 horses, 150 cavalry horses. Created in 210 B.C. for Emperor Qin Shi Huang. "Absolutely breathtaking." Don't believe mixed reviews.

### Beijing (北京)
Often plagued by pollution but essential. Forbidden City, walking streets, National Stadium, and the Great Wall. Recommended section: Mutianyu — less crowded, easy to find solitude. Off-the-beaten-path: hike from Badaling to Mutianyu and camp overnight on the wall.

### Tiger Leaping Gorge (虎跳峡, near Lijiang)
Definite must-see. Vertical cliffs, turquoise waters, Snow Dragon Snow Mountain, Jinsha River through terraced farmlands and limestone ridges. "One of the most stunning backdrops to be found anywhere in China."

## Must-Have Experiences

- **Order food with locals:** Best menus are in Chinese or "Chinglish." Try to eat with a local family — you may discover you enjoy duck's blood and ox tongue
- **Sichuan hot pot (火锅):** Dip thinly sliced meats, skewers, and vegetables into bubbling broth of herbs and spices. Best in Sichuan Province
- **Try to speak Mandarin:** Incredibly difficult language, but locals love your attempts. Learn a few phrases
- **Wander old towns:** Even in the most modern cities, pockets of old culture survive — narrow alleys, locals washing clothes in canals
- **Go to KTV (karaoke):** Huge part of Chinese culture. Rent a private room with drinks. Great way to meet locals
- **Eat something weird:** "Loads of crazy things on the menus here, but they almost always taste good"
- **Visit gardens:** Beautiful ancient architecture, escape from city noise and pollution
- **Tea houses:** Mingle with old men, try herbal drinks, get an ear cleaning (optional)
- **Play Mahjong (麻将):** Seen everywhere. "Chinese rummy" with porcelain tiles. Worth learning
- **Drink Baijiu (白酒):** Awful-tasting but the only way to bond with Chinese men at dinner

## Off the Beaten Path

- **Yangzhou (扬州):** Hardly any Western tourists. Old town, Slender West Lake Park, friendly inquisitive locals
- **Great Wall camping:** Hike from Badaling to Mutianyu and spend a night on the wall in a tent or under an outpost hut
- **Suzhou (苏州):** "Venice of the East" — beautiful canal town with a great old district, hardly any foreigners
- **Dacangzhen Valley (near Dali):** 30 minutes south of Dali. Yunnan's oldest Muslim communities. Welcoming locals, wonderfully off-track

## Transport

Excellent network of trains, buses, and taxis. Many trains are modern bullet trains reaching nearly 400 km/hour. Comfortable and affordable given the great distances. Taxi drivers are honest and always use the meter.

**Key tip:** Have your hostel write destinations in Mandarin. Hand the paper to ticket sellers instead of trying to pronounce city names — "you'll probably end up in the wrong place if you do!"

## Visa & Entry

- Tourist (L-Visa): Single-entry valid 30 days, must be used within 3 months. Double-entry valid within 6 months. Some nationalities can get up to 90 days
- **Requirements:** At least one blank passport page, passport valid for 6+ months from entry date
- **Hong Kong strategy:** Fly into Hong Kong (visa-free for most), apply for Chinese visa through hostel/hotel/guesthouse there. Need: 2 photos, passport photocopy, application forms
- No required vaccinations, no invitation letters

## Health

- Biggest risk: food poisoning and traveler's diarrhea (though restaurant sanitation is relatively high)
- Pack: antibiotics for diarrhea, Pepto-Bismol for upset stomachs, Tylenol for hangovers
- Keep medications in original marked containers for immigration

## Internet & Communication

- Hostels, hotels, and tourist restaurants have Wi-Fi
- Internet is censored (Facebook, Twitter, many blog sites blocked)
- Download a VPN before arriving
- Get a SIM card with data. Main providers: China Mobile, China Unicom
- Having a phone to call hostels or bilingual friends is essential for communication jams

## Practical Tips

- **Language barrier:** Very few people speak English outside hostels. Learn basic Mandarin phrases. Translation apps are essential. "This language barrier is part of what makes China so delightfully foreign"
- **Pollution:** Check the Air Quality Index daily; wear a mask when it looks smoggy. Mostly affects eastern China
- **Smoking:** Men smoke "before, during and after just about everything they do, both inside and out." Just accept it
- **Sanitation:** Cities are surprisingly clean, but children in split pants using public spaces as toilets is common
- **The people:** "Full of smiles and extremely honest." Taxi drivers use meters. Nobody tries to rip you off. Stares are from curiosity, not hostility
- **Best time:** Spring (March-May) or autumn (September-early November). Beijing is freezing in winter; Hong Kong gets typhoons July-September
- **Authors' verdict:** "China is one of the most underrated travelling destinations in the world!"
