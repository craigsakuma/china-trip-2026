# China Travel Guide

**Source:** Nick & Dariece — Goats on the Road (goatsontheroad.com). Long-running couple travel blog; lived and traveled in China for over a year, based in Yangzhou. Published Feb 2020, updated Jun 2023. First-hand experience across multiple seasons, budget-to-midrange perspective.

---

## Best Time to Visit China (春秋最佳 — Spring & Autumn Best)

**Ideal:** April–May and September–October. Best weather nationwide for sightseeing; avoids holiday crowds and extreme temperatures.

**Worst times / dates to avoid:**
- **Chinese New Year** (late Jan–mid Feb) — massive domestic travel
- **Labor Day Holiday** (May 1–3)
- **Chinese National Day / Golden Week** (Oct 1–7) — extreme crowding everywhere

**Climate notes:** North is extremely cold in winter; south is hot and humid in summer. Pack for all weather types regardless of season. China is technically a year-round destination — the authors experienced all four seasons living there.

## Visa & Entry Requirements (签证)

**Standard requirement:** Valid passport (6+ months remaining) + visa obtained in advance.

**Tourist visa (L visa):** Valid up to 30 days. Apply through Chinese embassy/consulate or visa agency. Start early — processing times vary.

**Required documents:**
- Valid passport with 6+ months validity
- Passport information page photocopy
- Completed application form
- One passport-style photo
- Proof of legal status (if not citizen of application country)
- Round-trip ticket or itinerary photocopy
- Hotel reservations or host contact information

**Visa-free entry (as of 2023 update):**
- 90 days: Armenia, Bosnia & Herzegovina, San Marino
- 60 days: Mauritius
- 30 days: Bahamas, Barbados, Belarus, Dominica, Ecuador, Fiji, Grenada, Qatar, Serbia, Suriname, Seychelles, Tonga, UAE
- 15 days (temporarily suspended): Brunei, Japan, Singapore

*Note: Check nearest Chinese embassy for current policies — visa-free list changes frequently.*

## Budget & Cost of Travel (旅行费用)

### Daily budgets (per person)
| Level | Yuan/day | USD/day | Notes |
|-------|----------|---------|-------|
| Budget | 200–300 | $14–28 | Hostels, street food, buses |
| Midrange | 350–900 | $50–130 | Decent hotels, some restaurants, trains |
| Top-end | 2,000+ | $285+ | Luxury hotels, fine dining |

### Accommodation costs
- **Hostels/budget hotels:** From 20 yuan ($3)/night in cheap areas; dorm beds from ~100 yuan ($14) in tourist zones
- **Midrange hotels:** ~200 yuan ($28)/night in smaller cities; more in Beijing/Shanghai
- **High-end hotels:** From 1,000 yuan ($143)/night
- **Airbnb:** Widely available, thousands of listings
- **Guesthouses:** Basic amenities, more authentic experience; book via Booking.com or Google Maps search

### Transportation costs
- **City buses:** 1–3 yuan per ride; city transport generally 10–30 yuan ($1.40–2.80)
- **Short train trips:** 4–9 yuan; longer journeys up to 50 yuan; intercity 400–900 yuan ($28–130)
- **Flights:** From ~600 yuan ($86) — most expensive option, budget travelers should avoid
- **Tuk-tuks:** Available for short city trips, watch for scams

### Food costs
- **Street food:** A few yuan per item
- **Restaurant meals:** 50–100 yuan ($7–14) typical; tourist areas up to 200 yuan ($28)+
- **Budget eating:** Under $5/meal easily achievable

### Entrance fees (examples)
- Great Wall: ~60 yuan ($9)
- Terracotta Warriors: ~110 yuan ($16)
- Museums: 10–20 yuan ($1.50–3)
- Free walking tours available in most major cities

## Getting Around China (交通)

**Train (火车):** Most popular intercity transport. Affordable, fast, reliable. Classes range from hard seat (cheapest) to soft sleeper (most comfortable). Book in advance, especially during holidays.

**Bus (公共汽车):** Cheapest option. Stops at every town. Can be crowded and uncomfortable during busy periods.

**Plane (飞机):** Quick between major cities but expensive. Limited daily flights on some routes.

**Bicycle (自行车):** Great for smaller towns and villages. Rent from hotels or local shops. Authors biked regularly in Yangzhou — decent bike lanes, felt safe. Heavy bicycle traffic in larger cities; drivers don't always watch for cyclists.

**Tuk-tuk (三轮车):** Common for short urban trips. Fun but watch for driver scams.

**Private driver:** Arrange through travel agencies, Uber, or Didi Chuxing (滴滴出行). Drivers generally speak English and can give local tips.

**Car rental:** Available in most cities. Learn local road rules — they differ from Western countries.

## Accommodation (住宿)

**Hotels:** Luxury options concentrated in Beijing and Shanghai. Budget chains (Holiday Inn, Best Western) throughout country. Many locally owned hotels offer good value.

**Hostels (青年旅社):** Authors' frequent choice — often preferred over hotels. "Boutique hostels" throughout the country offer better rooms plus social events: dumpling-making nights, meet-ups, pub crawls. Good backpacker vibe.

**Guesthouses (客栈):** Basic amenities (bed, desk, common area, sometimes kitchen). Self-service — no maid service. More authentic Chinese experience. Interact with owners and other guests.

**Airbnb:** Widely available with thousands of listings across the country.

**Home-sharing programs:** Stay with local families for most authentic experience.

## Food & Drink (美食)

### Regional cuisines
- **Northern China (北方):** Noodles, dumplings, buns, pancakes. Salty and savory flavors.
- **Southern China (南方):** Rice-based dishes, seafood, many sauces. Sweet and sour flavors.
- **Sichuan (四川):** Spicy with numbing Sichuan pepper (花椒 málà).

### Must-try dishes (authors' top 5 after 1+ year)

**Peking Duck (北京烤鸭):** Roasted duck served with thin pancakes, hoisin sauce, cucumbers. Sweet and savory, crispy skin. 700+ year history, originated in imperial palace. Found in nicer restaurants.

**Sichuan Hot Pot (四川火锅):** Spicy broth with beef, pork, lamb, chicken, Chinese cabbage, mushrooms, enoki, rice noodles. Numbing-spicy from Sichuan pepper. 1,000+ year history. Authors' favorite Sichuan experience.

**Dim Sum (点心):** Small steamed or fried portions — meats, seafood, vegetables, pastries. Salty, sweet, savory, sometimes spicy. Originated in Guangdong province. Found in Cantonese restaurants.

**Mapo Tofu (麻婆豆腐):** Spicy tofu with ground pork, bean sauce, chili pepper. Numbing-spicy Sichuan style. 1,000+ year Sichuan origin.

**Shanghai Dumplings (上海小笼包):** Pork and shrimp filling. Salty, umami, sweet. Found in Shanghai-style restaurants throughout China.

**Authors' tip:** Eat out with Chinese friends when possible — "they always order for us and never disappoint." Chinese food in China is vastly better than Chinese food in Western countries.

## Language (语言)

**Official language:** Mandarin (普通话), spoken by majority. 12+ dialects spoken across regions.

**English proficiency:** Limited outside major cities. Becoming more common in large urban areas. In authors' experience, "very little English spoken" outside major cities.

**Key strategy:** Before each travel day, have your hotel/hostel write your next destination, hotel name, and address in Chinese on a piece of paper. Show it to locals when needed — this saved the authors repeatedly.

**General attitude:** Chinese people are "very patient with visitors and don't expect you to know any of their languages." Hand motions and basic words go a long way.

---

## Currency & Money (货币)

**Currency:** Renminbi (RMB) / Chinese Yuan (¥). Divided into 100 cents (分).

**Credit cards:** Accepted in most large cities but always carry cash as backup.

## Culture & Etiquette (文化礼仪)

**Dress:** Chinese culture is generally conservative. Avoid revealing clothing. Avoid excessive public displays of affection.

**Chopstick rules (筷子礼仪):**
- Never stick chopsticks upright into a bowl of rice (resembles funeral incense — very bad omen)
- Lay chopsticks across bowl/plate while eating
- Place on table only when completely finished

**General approach:** Be respectful when speaking to locals. Learn basic Mandarin phrases before traveling. Ask questions to your hotel/hostel staff before setting off each day — saves enormous hassle.

## Top Destinations (必去城市)

**Beijing (北京):** Capital city. Forbidden City (紫禁城, 15th century, 500+ years as imperial home), Tiananmen Square, Temple of Heaven. Extensive restaurants and shops. Authors recommend a day tour of the Great Wall from Beijing.

**Yangshuo (阳朔):** In Guilin region. Dramatic karst landscapes, idyllic countryside. Hiking, biking, village exploration. Authors' favorite hiking destination in China.

**Sichuan (四川):** Stunning scenery, Buddhist temples, hot pot. Hiking, biking, village exploration.

**Xi'an (西安):** Terracotta Warriors — life-sized clay soldiers (2,000+ years old) guarding Emperor Qin Shi Huang's tomb. Thousands of statues. Must-see for history enthusiasts.

**Shanghai (上海):** The Bund waterfront — Art Deco buildings from 1920s–30s, best city views in China. Great photo location.

**Guilin (桂林) / Li River (漓江):** One of China's most beautiful natural areas. River flows through stunning countryside. Photographers' paradise.

**Great Wall (长城):** 5,500+ miles long. Authors took a day tour and "highly recommend it."

## Where to Stay by City

**Luxury hotels:** Best options in Beijing and Shanghai. High-end resorts in Hangzhou (杭州), Wuzhen (乌镇), Suzhou (苏州).

**Budget:** Affordable options everywhere. Low-cost chains and local hotels throughout the country.

## Practical Tips

- **Ask your accommodation staff everything** before setting off each day — they can write addresses in Chinese, explain transport, and save you from hassles
- **Get destination written in Chinese** on paper before every travel day — essential outside major cities
- **Book train tickets in advance** especially during holiday seasons
- **Avoid the three major holiday periods** (Chinese New Year, Labor Day, National Day) unless you enjoy massive crowds
- **Try "boutique hostels"** for social events and better rooms than basic hostels
- **Ride a bike** in smaller cities — authors found Yangzhou's bike lanes safe and convenient
- **Carry cash** even though credit cards work in big cities
- **Free walking tours** are available in most major cities — great for orientation and local knowledge
- **Pack for variable weather** — conditions change dramatically across China's regions
- **Eat street food fearlessly** — it's cheap, delicious, and everywhere
- **Don't be intimidated by the language barrier** — patience, hand gestures, and basic words work well
