# Ultimate China Survival Guide

**Source:** Richelle (adventuresaroundasia.com), lived in China for six years, speaks Chinese. Covers every practical aspect of China travel from visa logistics to street food strategy.

## Visas

**72-Hour Visa-Free Transit:** One city only, direct flights in and out, must exit through the same airport. Limited but useful for layovers.

**Tourist Visas:** Single, double, or multi-entry options. 30 days is the most common duration. Processing takes 3–4 days standard via consulate visit, FedEx services, or visa companies.

- **Cost:** $45–90 USD (Americans pay $140). Add $100+ for service/agency fees.
- **Cheaper option:** iVisa e-visas — $30 service fee, 15-day validity, no mailing your passport.

## Transportation — Within Cities

**Subways:** Best option. English maps and announcements in all major cities. Easy to navigate without Chinese.

**Buses:** Harder — stops display in pinyin and Chinese characters but require some reading ability. Cheap.

**Taxis:** Always insist on the meter. Get hotel business cards with addresses in Chinese characters to show drivers. Apps: Didi Dache (滴滴打车) or Kuaidi Dache — Chinese Uber equivalents.

**General tip:** Have your hotel write your destination in Chinese characters before heading out.

## Transportation — Long Distance

**Trains beat flights.** Stations are in city centers (vs. distant airports), no luggage restrictions, minimal delays. Example: Beijing–Xi'an is 4.4 hours by high-speed rail vs. 5.5+ hours when you factor in airport logistics for flights.

**Sleeper trains:** ~$70 for 24-hour journeys. Hard-sleeper (6-bunk compartment) recommended — cheaper, more social than soft sleeper. Best route: Xining to Lhasa.

**Flights:** Book via Skyscanner for budget domestic airlines. Useful for very long distances.

**Long-distance buses:** Cheapest option. Arrive 30 minutes early.

## Accommodation

**Budget (hostels):** Book on Hostelworld or Hostelz. Check reviews for English-speaking staff. Most accept cash or Chinese debit cards only. Budget 200 RMB ($30) deposit.

**Mid-range:** Agoda and Booking.com for boutique hotels. **Important:** Verify the hotel can host foreigners — some lack the required permit.

**Premium:** International chains or boutique options. The Opposite House in Beijing is a standout.

**Airbnb:** Growing options in China. Note: Airbnb owners must register guests with the local police.

**Beijing neighborhood picks:** Stay near Gulou (鼓楼), Andingmen (安定门), or Lama Temple (雍和宫) for the best hutong atmosphere.

**Beijing hostels:** Beijing Hutongren, Downtown Backpackers, Happy Dragon Courtyard.
**Beijing hotels:** The Orchid, 161 Lama Temple Courtyard.
**Beijing luxury:** The Opposite House.

## Internet & Censorship

**Blocked in China:** Facebook, Twitter/X, YouTube, Google (all services including Gmail), Instagram, Snapchat, Netflix, New York Times, sometimes BBC.

**Solution:** VPN is mandatory. Download **ExpressVPN** before arriving — most VPN download sites are blocked inside China. Install and test before you land.

## Language & Communication

**Essential apps:**
- **Pleco:** Offline Chinese dictionary. Type English, get pinyin + characters. Also reads handwritten characters via finger drawing. Works without internet.
- **Baidu Translate (百度翻译):** Works with wifi/data. Use instead of Google Translate (which is blocked).

**Strategy:** Have hotels write destinations in Chinese characters. Use picture menus at restaurants. Point at dishes. Show phone translations to staff. Learn basic Mandarin greetings.

## Food & Dining

**Where to eat:** Follow the locals. Crowded restaurants = quality and safety. Avoid empty tourist-trap restaurants.

**Spice warning:** Sichuan and Chongqing food is dramatically spicier than the rest of China. Carry Imodium. Chinese pharmacies sell stomach medicine.

**Universal safe orders:** Kung Pao chicken (宫保鸡丁), braised eggplant, dumplings (饺子), spinach with peanuts.

**Regional specialties:**
| Region | Dishes |
|--------|--------|
| **Sichuan** | Mapo tofu (麻婆豆腐), hot pot, mala liangfen (麻辣凉粉) |
| **Muslim/Xi'an** | Lamb kebabs (羊肉串), naan, dapanji (大盘鸡), roujiamo (肉夹馍) |
| **Beijing** | Peking duck (北京烤鸭), jianbing (煎饼) pancakes |

**Vegetarians:** Veggie-only meals are findable, though sauces may contain meat stock. Specify clearly.

**Street food:** Jianbing pancakes, BBQ skewers, thick hand-pulled noodles, seasonal roasted chestnuts. Safe because you watch preparation.

## Pollution

**For short-stay tourists:** Minimal concern. Grab **3M PM2.5 masks** on days when AQI exceeds 200.

**Monitoring apps:** AirVisual or Airpocalypse for real-time air quality data.

**Reality:** Beijing's bad days are intermittent and variable. Some other cities have more consistently mediocre air.

## Beijing Attractions — Quick Picks

**Great Wall:** Skip Badaling (too touristy). Best sections: Mutianyu (restored), Jinshanling (moderate crowds), Gubeikou (wild), Huanghuacheng (partially submerged).

**Historic sites:** Temple of Heaven, Summer Palace, Tiananmen Square, Jingshan Park (overlooks Forbidden City for 2 RMB).

**Hutong neighborhoods:** Andingmen, Gulou, Lama Temple area. Skip overcrowded Nanluoguxiang.

**Food tours:** UnTour (walking), Lost Plate (unlimited beer), Context Tours.

**Other:** 798 Art District, Houhai Lake, Bell and Drum Towers.

## Safety & General Tips

- Food poisoning is possible — eat at busy local spots, not tourist traps
- Hotels and hostels are helpful with directions and recommendations
- Register with local police if staying at an Airbnb long-term
- Keep accommodation receipts and room keys (refundable deposits are standard)
- Bring hotel business cards everywhere for taxi communication
