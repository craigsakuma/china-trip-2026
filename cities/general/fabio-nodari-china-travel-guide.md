# Complete Travel Guide to China

**Source:** Fabio Nodari (fabionodariphoto.com), Italian travel photographer living in China for many years. Published Jun 20, 2025. Updated Feb 8, 2026. All first-hand experience.

## Quick Facts

- **Size:** As large as Europe with comparable diversity. 56 officially recognized ethnic minorities. Every province has distinct cuisine and culture.
- **Safety:** Very safe. High number of police and security cameras. Scams rare compared to rest of Asia (taxi overcharging is most common). Robbery almost unheard of.
- **Language:** Very few people speak English, especially outside big cities like Shanghai. Microsoft Translator (works offline) and Pleco (best for Chinese characters) are essential apps.
- **Key reality:** Media portrayal is misleading. Chinese people are generally very curious and cordial toward foreigners. China is politically isolated, so Chinese don't know much about what happens outside and are genuinely curious.

## When to Visit

**Best:** Spring (especially Feb–May) and Autumn. February/March temperatures rising, flowers blooming (canola flowers in Luoping). April onward: rainy season begins.

**Avoid at all costs:** Chinese New Year, first week of May (Labor Holiday), first 10 days of October (National Holiday). August: peak domestic tourism.

**Summer:** Hot and humid everywhere except Tibet, Western Sichuan, and northern Yunnan. Expect huge crowds everywhere in August.

**Winter:** Freezing in the north (but heated indoors). South of the Yangtze: no heating systems, damp chill feels colder than actual temperature. Southern China (Hainan) is pleasant. Yunnan and Xishuangbanna are good winter options.

## Visa

Contact Chinese consulate or Visa For China for current requirements. Tourist visa requires hotel reservations, flight bookings, and detailed itinerary. Growing list of visa-free countries. Don't overstay — you can get in serious trouble.

## Travel Insurance

Chinese public hospitals: often chaotic, crowded, quick consultations, limited privacy. Competent and affordable care, but many prefer private/international hospitals for comfort and English-speaking staff. SafetyWing Nomad Insurance recommended — covers medical expenses, easy online signup even when abroad, monthly subscription model, includes travel delays and lost luggage.

## Hotels

Trip.com is best in English (more options, cheaper than Booking.com). Hard beds are the norm (Chinese medicine tradition). Cigarette smell common even in good hotels. No international breakfast in cheap hotels (expect soup noodles and rice). Some hotels refuse foreigners claiming "special license" requirement — this is not true. 4-5 star hotels have soft mattresses.

## Transportation

### Trains (Fabio's Favorite)

**Regular trains — ticket types:**
- **Hard seat:** Cheapest, most uncomfortable. Rows of non-reclining hard seats. Don't do this for long trips.
- **Hard sleeper:** Rows of 6 bunk beds per section, no doors. Choose lower/middle/upper. Most of the time Fabio's choice when HSR unavailable.
- **Soft sleeper:** Private rooms with 4 beds. Slightly more expensive. Ideal for groups of friends.
- **Soft sleeper (1-bed):** Single room. Available on some routes. Expensive.
- **Standing:** Same price as hard seat. Only if no other choice.

**HSR (高铁):** World's most extensive system (10,000+ km, first line opened 2007). Best for medium distances. Stations large and clean. Book via Trip.com.

**Ticket rules:** On sale 15 days before departure only. Fixed prices (no early bird savings). Free cancellation up to 48 hours before. No physical ticket needed — passport only (since Oct 2025).

### Flights
Best for long distances. Chinese airlines: OK, not amazing. Book on Trip.com. Websites often Chinese-only and don't accept foreign cards.

### Buses and Minivans
Often the only option in remote areas. Night buses have beds. Minivans replace public transport in remote areas — negotiate prices. Always bring passport for tickets. Foreigners must buy bus tickets in person (no online booking).

## Getting Around Cities

**AMAP:** Chinese equivalent of Google Maps, now available in English. Essential.
**Subways:** Most major cities. Shanghai's is longest in world, Beijing's busiest, Chongqing's most spectacular.
**Didi:** Chinese Uber, available in English. Much better than hailing taxis.
**Bike-sharing:** Available in most cities but requires Chinese phone number.
**Scooters:** Available near subway stations for short distances.

## Internet and Apps

### Essential Apps
- **WeChat (微信):** Messaging, payments (link credit card to WeChat Pay), built-in translation. Can't survive in China without it. May need existing user to scan QR code for activation.
- **Alipay (支付宝):** Payments (works with foreign cards), subway/bus/bike rental, translation features.
- **Didi:** Taxi app in English. Also available as mini-app inside WeChat and Alipay.
- **Trip.com:** Book everything (hotels, trains, flights). Only English option that works well.
- **Maps.me or Apple Maps:** Alternatives to blocked Google Maps.

### VPN (Essential)
Almost all foreign sites/apps are blocked. Download VPN before traveling. **ExpressVPN and NordVPN DO NOT WORK in China.**
- **Astrill:** Most popular, works well.
- **Let's VPN:** Cheap, works in China.
- **Mullvad:** Privacy-focused, cheap, works well.
- **Shadowsocks:** Advanced encryption protocol, cheap, for tech-savvy users only.

### SIM Options
- **eSIM (Holafly):** No VPN needed, but no Chinese phone number (needed for some apps).
- **Physical SIM:** Requires VPN but cheaper/faster internet, has phone number. Three carriers: China Unicom, China Telecom, China Mobile. Buy at store with passport.

## Food

Every province has distinct cuisine. 56 ethnic minorities each have characteristic dishes. Universal: noodles, hot pot, fried rice. Try street food but be careful about hygiene. If food touches the table, don't pick it up. Tea served instead of water in restaurants. No tipping.

**Celiac/food allergies:** China is rice-based in theory (good for celiac), but cross-contamination is common (same pots for rice and flour dishes). Serious intolerances: buy and cook ingredients yourself.

## Money

- **Cash:** Almost impossible to use practically. Everyone uses WeChat/Alipay. Even 100 RMB bill (highest denomination) causes change problems.
- **Currency exchange:** Only at Bank of China.
- **ATM:** Bank of China works best with foreign cards.
- **Credit cards:** Almost impossible to use directly. Link to WeChat Pay or Alipay.
- **Contact your bank** before leaving to prevent card blocks.

## Practical Tips

- **Always carry passport:** Used for trains, hotels, buying tickets, and sometimes entering attractions.
- **Gestures:** Chinese number gestures are completely different from Western ones (especially 6 and 10). Don't rely on hand signals.
- **Starting point for first-timers:** Yunnan is Fabio's favorite province and recommended starting region.
- **DIY vs agency:** For most destinations, DIY is fine and saves money. Exceptions: remote areas like Western Sichuan and Dulongjiang.
