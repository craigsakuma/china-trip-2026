# 36 Hours in Hong Kong

*By Tiffany May, The New York Times, Dec. 28, 2023*

## Key Stops

- **Sheung Wan** — Neighborhood dating to the mid-19th century, old shops selling tea and spices alongside some of the city's trendiest cafes.
- **Tai Kwun** — Former colonial prison compound restored as a public art complex with galleries, restaurants, bars and outdoor performance spaces.
- **M+** — Major art museum holding one of the world's most extensive collections of contemporary Chinese art, with expansive harbor views from its rooftop.
- **Sharp Island** — Verdant coastal area with easy hiking trails, pristine beaches, aquamarine waters and volcanic rocks.

---

## Friday

### 3:30 p.m. — Historic Shops in Sheung Wan

Time travel in Sheung Wan, a charming neighborhood where traditional stores sell tea and spices, just as they did more than a century ago when the city was a colonial trading outpost.

- **Cheung Hing Tea Hong** — Sells a wide variety of Chinese, European and Ceylon black teas, plus coffee beans. Its tea master, who has worked there for more than six decades, nimbly folds the shop's signature tieguanyin into an artful palm-size paper package using no tape or string.
- **Yuan Heng Spice Company** (est. 1912) — All manner of spices including Sichuan peppercorns, cinnamon bark and aged citrus peels. The shop's majestic cats are neighborhood fixtures.

*Tip: Check @hongkonghistoricalshops on Instagram for more gems.*

### 5 p.m. — Speakeasy in a Former Prison

**Tai Kwun** is a 19th-century prison and police station converted into a public arts compound in 2018. Seek out **001**, a speakeasy behind an unmarked black door hidden in a maze of walkways (staff will help you find it). Reward yourself with an Earl Grey martini. Use Tai Kwun's app for self-guided tours — some focus on architecture, others highlight the best photo spots.

### 7:30 p.m. — Dinner and Cocktails

**Nove at the Fringe** — Refined Cantonese dinner at the Fringe Club, a performing arts space in an oval brick building that served as a dairy warehouse in the 19th century. Try the watermelon in numbing mala chile sauce and honey-glazed char siu pork.

**Penicillin** — Sustainable cocktail bar that ferments food scraps and invents drinks using cacao husks, soy sauce and charred chive sprigs.

**Lockdown** — From the same owners, experimental versions of American Prohibition-era cocktails in an elegant space.

## Saturday

### 10 a.m. — Vegetarian Dim Sum at LockCha

Start with a stroll past ponds of koi, turtles and water lilies at **Hong Kong Park**, a 20-acre green space converted from British army barracks. Then visit **LockCha**, a tranquil teahouse on the ground floor of Flagstaff House Museum of Tea Ware (a Greek Revival mansion where the British forces commander once lived). Choose from more than 100 kinds of tea. LockCha's fully vegetarian dim sum menu is a rare treat — try the black truffle vegan siu mai, steamed custard buns and black fungus salad with sesame sauce.

### 12:30 p.m. — Contemporary Chinese Art at M+

**M+** opened in 2021 and holds more than 8,000 works in its permanent collection. The 700,000-square-foot museum, shaped like a giant inverted T, offers bold and nuanced critiques. Highlights include "Old People's Home" by Sun Yuan and Peng Yu (life-size wax figures resembling world leaders riding in motorized wheelchairs colliding into one another) and Fang Lijun's large painting of a man sinking into azure water. Visit the rooftop garden for an expansive harborfront view.

### 3 p.m. — Incense and Woodcarving in Yau Ma Tei

Stroll to **Yau Ma Tei**, a former fishing port where shops still sell incense and wood sculptures originally used by fishermen in protective rituals.

- **Tai On Coffee and Tea Shop** — Classic Cantonese diner with decor paying homage to famous Hong Kong film scenes, including a cardboard cutout from Wong Kar-wai's "In the Mood for Love." Try the egg tarts in unorthodox flavors. The owner has illustrated maps of nearby historic shops, available free.
- **Cheung Shing Fans Factory** — Incense sticks, essential oils and sandalwood fans.
- **Kwok Kee Wood Ware Sculpture** — Sixth-generation family business where artisans sit outside carving and painting figures traditionally used for ancestor worship.

### 4:30 p.m. — Sham Shui Po's Creative Hub

A wholesale garment district transformed into a creative neighborhood with vintage toy shops, vinyl stores and umbrella repair stalls.

- **Parallel Space** — Small independent gallery showing emerging Hong Kong artists with social commentary.
- **Book Punch** — Independent bookstore with literary fiction, poetry and children's books; staff give customers vegetables or snacks as thanks.
- **Bound Kowloon** — Neon-lit coffee shop and bar with a rebellious spirit, hosting concerts from punk rock to jazz.

### 6 p.m. — Sunset Hike at Garden Hill

An easy urban hike near Shek Kip Mei — ascend the stairs (about 10 minutes) from near YHA Mei Ho Youth Hostel to a flat area at the edge of the hill. The view reveals candy-colored residences including Shek Kip Mei Estate, Hong Kong's first public housing complex. In contrast to the glitzy skyline from Victoria Peak, this view conjures the city's working-class resilience. Popular among photographers and couples.

### 8:30 p.m. — Clay Pot Rice at Hing Kee

**Hing Kee** at Temple Street is a no-frills restaurant specializing in clay pot rice cooked over charcoal with toppings like Chinese sausage and seafood. Pour sweet soy sauce and enjoy the satisfying crunch of rice at the edge of the pot. The semi-outdoor restaurant sprawls across five storefronts. Cash only; expect a wait during peak times.

## Sunday

### 9 a.m. — Ferry to Sharp Island

Spend half a day exploring **Sharp Island**, a tranquil sanctuary northeast of the city center with verdant hiking trails, volcanic rocks and pristine beaches. The 60-to-90-minute journey is worth it for the beauty and relative privacy. At the public pier in Sai Kung, find a private boat operator, then pick up pineapple buns at Sai Kung Cafe and Bakery while you wait. Get off at Hap Mun Bay and follow signs for the hiking trail to Kiu Tsui Beach. When the tide is low, cross the Sharp Island Sand Levee — a path of monzonite boulders nicknamed "pineapple bun" rocks for their resemblance to the crust of the local pastry.

## Where to Stay

- **Regent Hong Kong** — Understated oasis on the harborfront. Restaurants include Nobu and two-Michelin-star Lai Ching Heen. Three rooftop infinity pools overlooking Victoria Harbor.
- **The Hari** — Stylish hotel with art-filled rooms curated by a London gallery. In-house Japanese and Italian restaurants, spacious gym with outdoor terrace.
- **Eaton HK** — Lively hotel hosting artistic and community events. Cinema, art gallery, Michelin-starred Chinese restaurant, large food court and rooftop bar.

## Getting Around

Hong Kong has one of the most easily navigable airports in the world — the airport express train reaches the city center in under 30 minutes. Get an **Octopus card** for the subway, buses and convenience stores. The MTR subway is air-conditioned with good cell service. Taxis are affordable (cash only). Uber is available but slightly more expensive.
