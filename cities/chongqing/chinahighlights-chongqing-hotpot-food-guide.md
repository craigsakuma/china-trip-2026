# Chongqing Hotpot & Food Guide — Birthplace of Chinese Hot Pot

**Source:** China Highlights (chinahighlights.com/chongqing/top-hotpot-restaurants.htm) with supplementary detail from ShinyVisa (shinyvisa.com/chongqing-food-guide/) and Trip.com. Comprehensive guide to Chongqing's hotpot culture and food scene beyond hotpot.

## Quick Facts

- **Chongqing is the birthplace of hotpot** — originally a dock workers' food at Yangtze River ports in the early 1900s. Workers stewed cheap offal (tripe, intestines, blood) in chili oil to make it palatable.
- **Flavor profile:** Málà (麻辣) — numbing Sichuan peppercorns + fiery dried chilies. Chongqing hotpot uses beef tallow (牛油) base, making it richer and spicier than Sichuan-style (which often uses vegetable oil).
- **Chongqing vs Sichuan hotpot:** Chongqing = beef tallow, heavier, more offal-focused. Sichuan = lighter, fresher vegetable oil base.
- **Budget:** Hotpot ¥70-100/person at local restaurants; ¥100-500 at upscale venues. Street noodles ¥8-15.
- **"Michelin" philosophy inverted:** In Chongqing, poor ambiance often signals superior taste. Noise and crowds are marks of authenticity.

## Top Hotpot Restaurants (火锅店)

### Yuwei Xiaoyu Hot Pot (渝味晓宇火锅) — The Classic
**Address:** No. 86 Pipa Hill Street, Yuzhong District (渝中区枇杷山正街86号).
**Price:** ¥70-100/person. **Phone:** +86 23 6351 2888.
Featured on CCTV's "A Bite of China" (舌尖上的中国). Ranked first among Chongqing's top 50 hotpot restaurants. Authentic, down-to-earth atmosphere — plastic stools, fluorescent lights, incredible food. Strong beef tallow base, heavy on the spice. Best for adventurous eaters already familiar with Chinese food.
**Nearby:** Crown Escalator (皇冠大扶梯), Tongyuan Gate, Fairy Cave Street.

### Pipa Garden Shiweixian Hot Pot (枇杷园食为鲜火锅) — The Spectacle
**Address:** Jinzhu Village, Nanshan Street, Nan'an District (南岸区南山街道金竹村).
**Price:** ~¥100/person. **Phone:** +86 23 6248 3138.
World's largest hotpot restaurant (Guinness record). 600+ tables spanning an entire mountainside. Lights strung from foot to summit create a glittering hillside visible across the city — one of Chongqing's most iconic nighttime scenes. Mostly outdoor seating. Book second or third floor for the best panoramic city views. The food is solid; the experience is unforgettable.

### Underground City Old Hot Pot (地下城老火锅) — The Bunker
**Address:** Underground City, No. 5 Ji'an Road, Longta Street, Yubei District.
**Price:** ~¥100/person. **Phone:** +86 23 6219 0123.
Operates inside 1950s-era air-raid shelters — 60,000 square meters of interconnected underground tunnels. Vintage decor with Cultural Revolution-era slogans. **Open 24 hours.** The longest tunnel stretches 520 meters. Pro tip: choose seating near the restrooms — the walk can be epic otherwise.

### Sister Pei's Old Hot Pot (裴姐老火锅) — Hongya Cave Branch
**Address:** 11th Floor, Hongya Cave, Cangbai Road, Jiefangbei, Yuzhong District.
**Price:** ~¥100/person. **Phone:** +86 23 6383 1777.
Perpetually packed with long wait lines. Signature dishes: rainbow beef (彩虹牛肉) and cherry blossom beef (樱花牛肉). Some basic English support. Major advantage: direct access to Hongya Cave's illuminated night views after your meal.

### Yinghua Crossing Hot Pot (樱花渡火锅) — The Luxury Option
**Address:** Changjia Hui, Nanbin Road (also Sandong Bridge location).
**Price:** ¥100-500/person.
Called the "Hermès of Chongqing hotpot." High-end design merging modern art with traditional culture. Premium Wagyu, fresh seafood, handmade meatballs, organic vegetables. Both locations offer excellent river confluence views. For travelers who want the hotpot experience without the gritty local ambiance.

## How to Eat Chongqing Hotpot — A Guide

### Choosing Your Pot
- **Full spicy (全辣):** Entire pot is fiery red chili oil. Only for the brave.
- **Yuan Yang pot (鸳鸯锅, "Mandarin Duck"):** Half spicy red, half mild white broth (mushroom, tomato, or bone soup). The recommended choice for mixed-tolerance groups.
- **"Mildly spicy" (微辣):** Still intensely hot by Western standards. Do not underestimate.

### The Classic Dipping Sauce
Traditional Chongqing formula: **garlic paste (蒜泥) + sesame oil (香油) + a pinch of salt**. That's it. Customize by adding cilantro, green onions, vinegar, or oyster sauce from the condiment station. The sesame oil cools the chili burn.

### What to Order
**Must-try items:**
- Fresh tripe (鲜毛肚) — dip in boiling oil for 15-20 seconds. The signature Chongqing hotpot ingredient.
- Duck intestine (鸭肠) — similar quick-cook technique. Crispy when done right.
- Sliced beef (牛肉) — various cuts including the rainbow and cherry blossom versions at premium restaurants.
- Tofu skin (豆皮) — absorbs the broth flavor.
- Lotus root (莲藕) and potato slices (土豆片) — mild counterpoints.

**Spice relief items:** Brown sugar glutinous rice cakes (红糖糍粑), iced jelly (冰粉), sour plum soup (酸梅汤). Order these alongside your hotpot.

### Etiquette
- Shared pot — communal eating. Use serving chopsticks to add raw items; personal chopsticks to eat.
- Quick-cook items (tripe, intestine) go in for 15-20 seconds. Vegetables and meatballs cook 3-5 minutes.
- The broth intensifies over the meal as it reduces. Later dishes will be spicier.
- Chongqing locals often eat hotpot in summer — they believe sweating aids detoxification.

## Beyond Hotpot (非火锅美食)

### Chongqing Xiao Mian (重庆小面) — The Breakfast Noodle
Springy wheat noodles in chili oil with minced garlic, soy sauce, green onions, preserved vegetables (榨菜), and sometimes ground pork or peas. Many locals eat xiao mian for breakfast at sidewalk noodle shops. ¥8-15 per bowl. Order "gān liū" (干溜, dry-tossed) for maximum chili oil absorption.

### Suan La Fen (酸辣粉) — Hot & Sour Noodles
Sweet potato glass noodles in a tangy chili-vinegar broth with peanuts and preserved vegetables. The sour-spicy combination is addictive. Street stall staple.

### Grilled Fish (万州烤鱼)
Whole fish grilled over charcoal then simmered in a trough of chili oil with bean sprouts, lotus root, and tofu skin. Originally from Wanzhou district. A group-dining dish.

### Mao Xue Wang (毛血旺)
Hearty spicy stew with duck blood cubes (it sounds worse than it tastes), tripe, luncheon meat, and bean sprouts in málà broth. Quintessential Chongqing comfort food.

### La Zi Ji (辣子鸡) — Chili Chicken
Deep-fried chicken pieces buried under a mountain of dried red chilies and Sichuan peppercorns. You pick out the chicken; the chilies are for flavor, not eating. Dramatic presentation.

### Chuan Chuan Xiang (串串香) — Skewer Hot Pot
Customizable meat, seafood, and vegetable skewers cooked in shared spicy broth. Pay by counting bamboo sticks. Less commitment than full hotpot, good for sampling many items.

### Douhua (豆花) — Tofu Pudding
Silky fresh tofu pudding served savory with chili oil and crispy fried dough. A mild-spice option and traditional Chongqing breakfast.

### Spicy Rabbit Head (麻辣兔头)
Braised whole rabbit heads in chili sauce, served cold. An acquired taste and a conversation starter. Common bar snack.

## Where to Eat — Neighborhoods

- **Jiefangbei (解放碑):** Central dining hub. Tourist-friendly with diverse price ranges.
- **Nanbin Road (南滨路):** Riverside restaurants with city skyline views. Mid-range to upscale.
- **Shibati (十八梯):** Historic tiered neighborhood now renovated. Street food and atmosphere.
- **Ciqikou (磁器口):** Ancient town with traditional snacks. Touristy but good for sampling.
- **Guanyinqiao (观音桥):** Local shopping district with excellent food streets. Less tourist traffic.

## Practical Tips

- **Spice management:** Sesame oil dipping sauce is your best friend. Peanut milk (花生奶) from convenience stores also helps neutralize capsaicin.
- **Clothing warning:** Hotpot steam will saturate your clothes with chili oil aroma. Wear something you do not mind smelling like a spice market for a day.
- **Solo dining:** Maocai (冒菜) is the solo-friendly version of hotpot — one-person servings with pre-cooked ingredients.
- **Late-night eating:** Chongqing's food scene runs late. BBQ stalls, noodle shops, and some hotpot restaurants operate past midnight or 24 hours.
- **"Watch what locals eat":** Restaurants full of Chongqing natives are the ones worth queuing for. Empty tourist restaurants with English menus are usually mediocre.
