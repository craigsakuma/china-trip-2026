# 36 Hours in Beijing

*By Justin Bergman, The New York Times, Feb. 18, 2015*

## Friday

### 1. Beads and Incense | 4 p.m.

In the new Beijing of star-architect towers and gleaming Porsches, the Lama Temple (Yonghe Temple) stands as a reminder of a less material and more spiritual time. Built as a prince's home in the 17th century, the complex was gradually transformed into a lamasery and is today one of the most active and colorful Buddhist temples in the city. Beijingers pray amid gnarled pine trees with burning joss sticks held aloft, while monks offer quiet blessings in hidden corners. Outside the lovely ginkgo-lined entrance are shops crammed with Buddhist trinkets and incense, the sounds of Tibetan music floating down the street.

### 2. Beijing Brews | 6 p.m.

The traditional hutongs around the Lama Temple are a fantastic place to soak up Beijing street life. Avoid overly gentrified Nanluoguxiang and head instead to the maze of alleys around quieter Baochao Hutong to the west. Here, locals gather for nightly mahjong games and sip beer at dumpling shops beneath gray-tiled rooftops. Great Leap Brewing, a pioneer in Beijing's craft beer scene, makes unique ales like Iron Buddha Blonde infused with tea from the mountains of Fujian province. Nearby Slow Boat Brewery Taproom has more than a dozen unpasteurized beers on tap.

### 3. The Spice Is Right | 8 p.m.

It's brightly lit and raucous — Beijing may have more refined Sichuan restaurants these days, but Chuan Ban has retained a loyal following among locals because of its authentically mouth-numbing food, run by the Sichuan provincial government. Standouts include mapo doufu (cubes of tofu swimming in crimson chile oil smothered in ground Sichuan peppercorns) and stir-fried shrimp balls heaped with ground pork, preserved vegetables and diced chiles.

## Saturday

### 4. Gentle Gentrification | 11 a.m.

The Dashilar neighborhood, one of the oldest in Beijing, is following a sustainable gentrification path with architects and designers working to retain the fabric of the community. One former factory in a 1950s Art Deco building has been renovated into a cafe, Spoonful of Sugar, serving organic coffee from Yunnan province. Down the lane, check out the Ubi Gallery for handmade designer jewelry and Chinese ceramics, and the Li+U Workshop for handsome leather bags and wallets made on-site.

### 5. Fit for a King | 1 p.m.

Old Beijing Zhajiang Noodle King does one thing really well: zha jiang mian, a hand-pulled wheat noodle dish just as evocative of Beijing as roast duck or hearty dumplings. Waiters dash up and down stairs shouting orders while diners crowd around wooden tables slurping bowls of noodles topped with shredded cucumber and radish, bean sprouts and a tangy sauce made with minced pork and fermented soybean paste.

### 6. Suburban Cool | 3 p.m.

The 798 Art District is still the hub of Beijing's contemporary art scene, though commercialization has brought souvenir shops and cafes. Head farther afield to the suburbs beyond the Fifth Ring Road for experimental art in less-crowded spaces. The Three Shadows Photography Art Center, designed by Ai Weiwei, is said to be the first gallery in China devoted to photography. The Red Brick Art Museum focuses on video and installation works, with a tranquil Chinese garden and angular modernist building.

### 7. Duck Dressed Up | 8 p.m.

Duck de Chine succeeds in elevating roast duck to a higher plane. Set in a renovated factory with exposed beams and elegant red lanterns, this is the kind of place with a gong to announce the arrival of your bird. The duck is crisped in an oven with date wood to enhance the flavor, then carved tableside and wrapped in a pancake with house-made tian mian jiang (sweet bean sauce) and radish, celery and leek slivers. Reservations are a must; it takes over an hour to cook.

### 8. Hutong Hooch | 10 p.m.

At Capital Spirits, a hutong speakeasy with antique wooden furniture and no sign on the door, a mostly young crowd sips baijiu (grain alcohol) from a menu that varies by strength and flavor. The bar infuses its own pomegranate, garlic and Sichuan peppercorn baijius.

## Sunday

### 9. Parks and Recreation | 8 a.m.

The Forbidden City can feel overwhelming with the sheer number of courtyards and pushy tourists. A far more peaceful alternative on the weekend is the Summer Palace, the imperial family's former country escape. It's easy to ditch the crowds by hiking the pine-shaded hill behind the palace and taking in the view of the lake below. Follow the sounds of music — you might find a choir belting out patriotic tunes with a brass band, or a Peking Opera soloist performing in a pagoda for an audience of one.

### 10. Designer Dumplings | 1 p.m.

The Opposite House hotel exemplifies the bold new architectural aesthetic taking root in Beijing. The dim sum menu at its Jing Yaa Tang restaurant is a real bargain — fluffy barbecued pork buns, pan-fried turnip cakes and homemade walnut milk. The showstopper is a 19-foot-tall, 5,000-plus-drawer modern take on a traditional apothecary cabinet.

## Lodging

- **Fairmont Beijing** — 222 rooms, excellent English-speaking service, short taxi ride from the Forbidden City, Sanlitun and the Temple of Heaven. Gold Rooms offer great views from the 20th floor skybridge.
- **Grace Hotel** — 30-room boutique hotel in a former factory inside the 798 Arts District. High ceilings, wooden floors, and brightly colored artworks.
