# NYT 36 Hours in Beijing (北京) — Weekend Itinerary

## Lama Temple (雍和宫 Yōnghé Gōng) — Active Buddhist Temple in Northeastern Beijing

The Yonghe Temple (Lama Temple) is one of Beijing's most active and colorful Buddhist temples, built as a Qing prince's home in the 17th century before conversion into a Tibetan Buddhist lamasery. Beijingers pray among ancient gnarled pine trees with joss sticks, while monks offer blessings in quieter corners. The ginkgo-lined entrance street is lined with shops selling Buddhist trinkets and incense with Tibetan music overhead. **Recommended: 4 p.m. Friday for atmosphere.** Metro: Line 2/5, Yonghegong Station.

## Hutong Craft Beer — Baochao Hutong (宝钞胡同) Near Lama Temple

Skip the overly gentrified Nanluoguxiang (南锣鼓巷) and head west to the quieter alleys around **Baochao Hutong (宝钞胡同)** where locals play mahjong and drink beer at dumpling shops under gray-tiled rooftops. Two standout bars: **Great Leap Brewing** — a craft beer pioneer with unique ales like Iron Buddha Blonde (brewed with Fujian mountain tea). **Slow Boat Brewery Taproom** — a dozen+ unpasteurized beers on tap. Both are hutong institutions with strong local followings.

## Sichuan Food — Chuan Ban (川办) Government-Run Restaurant

**Chuan Ban** is run by the Sichuan provincial government and draws a loyal local crowd for its authentically mouth-numbing Sichuan food. Standouts: mapo doufu (麻婆豆腐 — silken tofu in crimson chile oil with ground Sichuan peppercorns) and stir-fried shrimp balls with ground pork, preserved vegetables, and diced chiles. Brightly lit and raucous — not refined, but genuinely spicy and local. **Recommended: Friday dinner.**

## Dashilar (大栅栏) — Sustainable Gentrification in Old Beijing

One of Beijing's oldest neighborhoods in the southern city center, Dashilar has undergone careful gentrification preserving community fabric. A 1950s Art Deco former factory houses **Spoonful of Sugar**, a cafe serving organic Yunnan-province coffee. Nearby: **Ubi Gallery** for handmade designer jewelry and Chinese ceramics; **Li+U Workshop** for leather bags and wallets made on-site. More artisan and less commercial than the hutong tourist trail. **Recommended: Saturday morning.**

## Zha Jiang Mian (炸酱面) — Old Beijing Zhajiang Noodle King (老北京炸酱面大王)

**Old Beijing Zhajiang Noodle King** specializes in zha jiang mian — hand-pulled wheat noodles topped with shredded cucumber, radish, bean sprouts, and a tangy sauce of minced pork and fermented soybean paste (黄豆酱). Waiters shout orders up and down stairs while diners crowd wooden tables. As iconic to Beijing as Peking duck. **Recommended: Saturday lunch.**

## Contemporary Art Beyond 798 — Three Shadows & Red Brick Museums

The **798 Art District (798艺术区)** remains Beijing's contemporary art hub but commercialization has diluted the experience. For serious art beyond the Fifth Ring Road: **Three Shadows Photography Art Center (三影堂摄影艺术中心)** — designed by Ai Weiwei, China's first gallery dedicated to photography. **Red Brick Art Museum (红砖美术馆)** — video and installation art in a modernist angular building with a tranquil traditional Chinese garden. Less crowded, more experimental. **Recommended: Saturday afternoon.**

## Peking Duck — Duck de Chine (全鸭季) Elevated Version

**Duck de Chine** is considered the upscale benchmark for Peking duck in Beijing. Set in a renovated factory with exposed beams and red lanterns, the duck is crisped with date wood for enhanced flavor, then carved tableside and wrapped in pancakes with house-made tian mian jiang (甜面酱 sweet bean sauce), radish, celery, and leek. **Reservations essential** — cooking takes over an hour. A gong announces the arrival of your bird. **Recommended: Saturday dinner.**

## Baijiu Bar — Capital Spirits Hutong Speakeasy

**Capital Spirits** is a hutong bar with no sign on the door and antique wooden furniture. Menu organized by strength and flavor profile. House-infused baijiu (白酒 grain alcohol) varieties include pomegranate, garlic, and Sichuan peppercorn. Mostly young local crowd. The rare place to experience baijiu as a craft spirit rather than a shot-glass obligation. **Recommended: Saturday night.**

## Summer Palace (颐和园 Yíhéyuán) — Escape from Forbidden City Crowds

The **Summer Palace** is the imperial family's former country retreat and far more peaceful than the Forbidden City on weekends. Escape the main crowds by hiking the pine-shaded hill behind the palace for views over Kunming Lake. Follow sounds of music through the grounds — weekend mornings bring brass-band choirs singing patriotic songs and Peking Opera soloists performing in pavilions. Entry: ~30 RMB. Metro: Line 4, Xiyuan Station. **Recommended: Sunday morning.**

## Dim Sum — Jing Yaa Tang (京雅堂) at The Opposite House Hotel

**The Opposite House** hotel represents Beijing's bold contemporary architecture. Its restaurant **Jing Yaa Tang (京雅堂)** serves a dim sum menu that punches above its price point: fluffy barbecued pork buns (叉烧包), pan-fried turnip cakes (萝卜糕), and homemade walnut milk. The centerpiece is a 19-foot-tall cabinet with 5,000+ drawers reinterpreting a traditional Chinese apothecary. **Recommended: Sunday lunch.** Located in Sanlitun (三里屯).

## Hotels Mentioned

- **Fairmont Beijing** — 222 rooms, strong English-speaking service, Gold Rooms with skybridge views from floor 20. Short taxi from Forbidden City, Sanlitun, Temple of Heaven.
- **Grace Hotel** — 30-room boutique inside the 798 Arts District in a former factory. High ceilings, wooden floors, art-filled rooms. For visitors prioritizing the contemporary art scene.

## Practical Tips

- **Lama Temple:** Arrive before 5 p.m. closing; busiest on weekends and Buddhist holidays
- **Duck de Chine:** Book same-day by phone; walk-ins often turned away after 7 p.m.
- **Summer Palace:** Go early (8 a.m.) to beat tour groups; the back hill is almost always quiet
- **Dashilar:** 10-minute walk from Qianmen Station (Line 2); combine with Qianmen pedestrian street
- **Baijiu warning:** Capital Spirits' strongest bottles approach 60% ABV — pace accordingly
- **2015 caveat:** Some specific venues (Spoonful of Sugar, certain hutong bars) may have changed; the neighborhoods and cuisine recommendations remain accurate
