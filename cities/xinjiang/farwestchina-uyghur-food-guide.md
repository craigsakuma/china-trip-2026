# Uyghur Food — Essential Xinjiang Cuisine Guide (维吾尔美食)

**Source:** Far West China (farwestchina.com) by Josh Summers, long-term Xinjiang resident. Covers the top 10 Uyghur dishes with ordering tips, regional context, and street food culture across Xinjiang's bazaars and night markets.

## Quick Facts

- **Cuisine character:** Central Asian-influenced — lamb, cumin, hand-pulled noodles, flatbread. More Turkish/Uzbek than Chinese.
- **Dominant protein:** Lamb and mutton. Beef also common. Pork is absent (Halal dietary laws).
- **Key spices:** Cumin (孜然), black pepper, chili flakes, sesame.
- **Dining culture:** Shared plates, bazaar stalls, and night markets. Dishes designed for communal eating.
- **Multi-ethnic:** Encompasses Uyghur, Hui (回族), Kazakh (哈萨克族), and Tajik (塔吉克族) culinary traditions.

## The Essential 10 Dishes

### 1. Polo — Uyghur Pilaf (抓饭/Zhuāfàn)

Rice, carrots, and lamb slow-cooked in oil. The most popular local dish — available at every restaurant and street stall. Order by saying "polo." Culturally significant: a dish of celebration and communal sharing, symbolizing unity and hospitality. Sometimes topped with raisins and dried apricots.

### 2. Laghman — Hand-Pulled Noodles (拌面/Bànmiàn)

Hand-stretched noodles covered with stir-fried vegetables (tomatoes, peppers, onions) and meat. Shared specialty between Uyghur and Hui cuisines. Originated along the Silk Road's Hexi Corridor. The noodle-pulling technique is mesmerizing to watch — many restaurants pull fresh to order.

### 3. Lamb Kebabs (烤肉串/Kǎo ròu chuàn)

Grilled lamb seasoned with cumin and pepper. A dietary staple highlighting Xinjiang's exceptional meat quality. Available at every street corner, night market, and restaurant. Charcoal-grilled for smoky flavor. Fat pieces interspersed between meat for richness.

### 4. Uyghur Bread — Nan (馕/Náng)

Multiple forms including flatbread and bagel-like varieties. Central Asian tradition with local adaptations. Baked in a tandoor (tonur) oven. Varieties include sesame-topped, onion-filled, and plain. Staple accompaniment to every meal. Some nan weighs over 1 kg.

### 5. Samsa — Baked Pastries (烤包子/Kǎo bāozi)

Lamb-and-onion filled pastries baked in a traditional tandoor oven until crispy golden outside, soft and flavorful inside. Triangle-shaped, sometimes round. Portable snack ideal for travel. Described as "a grilled Hot Pocket" but far better.

### 6. DaPanJi — Big Plate Chicken (大盘鸡/Dà pán jī)

Whole chicken with potatoes, onions, peppers, and vegetables served on one large plate. A Hui dish adopted across Xinjiang. Designed for sharing — sop up the sauce with hand-pulled noodles or flatbread. Feeds 2–4 people. One of the best-value dishes.

### 7. Uyghur Ice Cream — Maruxna (玛鲁兹纳)

Summer street cart treat differing from Western ice cream — denser, creamier texture. Only a few kuai per serving. Multiple flavor options. Look for vendors with decorated carts throughout bazaars.

### 8. Stuffed Lamb Intestine (米肠子/面肺子)

Rice and lamb mixture stuffed inside intestine, often served alongside lamb lung. An acquired taste but beloved by locals. Usually available at dedicated stalls in bazaars.

### 9. Matang — Nut Brittle (玛糖)

Street-sold nut snack featuring walnuts, almonds, and sesame in a sweet crispy binding. Bought by the slice at bazaar stalls. Portable, shelf-stable, and available in many varieties. Makes a good souvenir.

### 10. Xinjiang Fruits (新疆水果)

Xinjiang residents consume more fruit per capita than other Chinese regions. The dry climate and extreme temperature swings produce exceptionally sweet fruit:
- **Hami melon (哈密瓜):** From Hami, the original honeydew.
- **Turpan grapes (吐鲁番葡萄):** From Grape Valley — seedless, intensely sweet.
- **Korla pears (库尔勒香梨):** Crisp, fragrant, and juicy.
- **Dried fruits:** Figs, apricots, raisins — sold by weight at every bazaar.

## Regional Specialties by City

- **Kashgar:** Sunday market food stalls, century-old teahouse, sheep-head soup.
- **Turpan:** Grape products, roasted dumplings, Uyghur noodle soup.
- **Urumqi:** International Grand Bazaar food court, Hui Muslim quarter, night markets.
- **Hotan (和田):** Desert rose tea, dried fruits, yogurt drinks.

## Bazaar & Night Market Culture

Food is central to Xinjiang's bazaar experience. Every town has a bazaar with dedicated food sections. Night markets fire up around 8–9 PM (Xinjiang time) with kebab smoke, noodle stations, and fruit vendors. Urumqi's Grand Bazaar and Kashgar's food streets are the most famous.

## Practical Tips

- Point-and-order is common — language barriers are manageable at food stalls.
- Prices are low: most dishes 15–40 RMB. Kebabs 2–5 RMB each.
- Halal (清真) dining is the norm — almost all Uyghur restaurants are pork-free.
- Tea is served automatically and continuously — it's part of the hospitality.
- Nan bread is best eaten fresh and warm from the tandoor.
- Big Plate Chicken is designed for sharing — don't order one per person.
- Xinjiang operates on Beijing time officially but local life runs 2 hours later.
- Bring wet wipes — many dishes are eaten with hands (polo, lamb).
